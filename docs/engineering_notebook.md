# Engineering Notebook: Transformers for Edit Distance and Error Correction

This is the working record of the project, written as it actually unfolded. It covers three architectures, two clean results, one failure, and the reasoning that connected them. Numbers come from `ctc/history.txt`, the logs in `ctc/`, the plots in `seq/` and `plots/`, and the diagnostic runs described below.

## The goal

The brief was simple to state and hard to satisfy. Take error correction code sequences, and see how far a Transformer can go on two fronts: estimating the edit distance between sequences, and recovering the original bits of a corrupted codeword. The yardstick was a set of classical methods, including the BCJR decoder and the CGK and CNN-ED edit-distance baselines. The catch, which took a while to fully respect, is that the two fronts demand different outputs. Estimating a distance needs a number. Recovering bits needs a sequence. A model that does one cannot always be scored on the other.

## Phase one: generate the sequence

The first design was the obvious one. An autoregressive sequence-to-sequence Transformer with a decoder, trained to print the corrected sequence one bit at a time under a positional binary cross-entropy loss. It trained, and at first glance it looked healthy. When its generated sequences were compared to the targets by edit distance, the predictions tracked the diagonal across the whole range. That is the plot in `seq/distance_scatter.png`, and it is worth a Pearson correlation of 0.8589 and a Spearman of 0.8557. The RMSE came in at 2.4817, the MAE at 1.9312. A Principal Component Analysis of the encoder states, `seq/pca_clusters.png`, held 86.3 percent of the variance in two components and laid the sequences out by edit distance. The encoder had clearly learned something real about the structure.

Then came the number that ended the phase. The Block Error Rate was 0.9999. Almost no block was recovered without at least one wrong bit, even though the Bit Error Rate was only 0.3716. The two numbers together are the whole story. The model got most bits right but almost never got a full block right, which is the signature of a shift. Error correction involves insertions and deletions. Delete one bit early in a sequence and every later bit slides one position to the left. The positional loss compares position by position, so it reads a one-operation edit as dozens of errors and punishes a sequence that is, in edit terms, nearly correct. The decoder spent its training fighting a loss that misread its near-misses as disasters.

There was a second, smaller lesson in this phase. A variant tried to skip generation and regress the edit distance directly as a single scalar. That version saturated. It predicted around five operations and stopped, ignoring anything larger, which is visible as the flat ceiling in `seq/scatter_plot.png` and shows up as a weak 0.311 correlation and a 3.772 RMSE. The direct read of the distance was worse than the read taken from the generated sequence. So the fair numbers for this phase are the sequence-based ones.

## Phase two: stop generating, start measuring

If generation is what invites the shift penalty, then the fix is to stop generating. The decoder came off, and the encoder was repurposed as a Siamese network. Each sequence goes through the same encoder and lands as a point in a 256-dimensional space, and two sequences are compared by the straight-line distance between their points. There is no alignment to get wrong because there is no sequence being produced.

The encoder kept its shape: embedding dimension 256, eight attention heads, four layers, with mean pooling to collapse the per-position output into one vector. Training used a Triplet Margin Loss over an anchor, a correlated positive, and a corrupted negative, for 30 epochs with AdamW and a margin of 1.0. To put the results on the edit-distance scale, a one-dimensional linear regression mapped embedding distance to true Levenshtein distance, which is a calibration, not a second model.

This worked. Correlated pairs landed at an average distance of 2.7128 and corrupted pairs at 3.3750, for a Triplet Accuracy of 0.7238. The separation is the histogram in `plots/distance_histogram.png`. The RMSE dropped to 2.092 and the MAE to 1.670, the best absolute error of any learned model in the comparison, ahead of CGK at 2.622 and CNN-ED at 2.906. The price was paid in the metrics that need bits. With no sequence coming out, Hamming Loss and Bit Error Rate simply do not apply. One more honest wrinkle: the Pearson and Spearman correlations fell to roughly 0.45, below the convolutional and alignment baselines. Triplet loss pushes each pair past a fixed margin, which tightens local ordering and absolute error, but it never asks the entire severity scale to line up in rank. Good distances, weaker ranking.

## Phase three: get the bits back without the shift

Phase two was a good metric model and a dead end for bit-level scoring. Phase three tried to have both. The idea was a Connectionist Temporal Classification head on top of the same encoder. This loss scores every possible alignment between input and output at once, using a blank symbol to absorb spacing, so it tolerates insertions and deletions without the position-by-position penalty that sank phase one. On paper it is the right tool for a channel that shifts.

It carried one hard requirement. The loss must place a blank between any two identical adjacent output symbols, and binary codewords are full of repeated bits. With an input and a target both of length 100, there is no room in the lattice to insert those blanks, and the backend stalls. The fix was to upsample the encoder output by a factor of two with nearest-neighbor interpolation, stretching the lattice to length 200. That gave the blanks somewhere to go. The decode side needed its own machinery, so a greedy decoder was written to collapse repeats and strip blanks, followed by a length-aware Levenshtein alignment to score Bit Error Rate, Block Error Rate, and Hamming Loss when the predicted and true lengths differ. The decoder and the alignment were checked against synthetic cases first and behaved correctly.

The plan was clean. The execution exposed every assumption in turn.

### Getting onto the GPU

The model was developed and verified on a machine with no GPU, so everything was first checked on the CPU against the small 45 megabyte triplet file. The loss came down on that file, which confirmed the loss path and the per-sample input lengths were wired correctly. The real training had to move to a lab server with an RTX 3060, and that move was its own small adventure. The code went up through GitHub, but the first clone landed on the wrong branch and the new files were missing until the checkout switched to the training branch. The 4.5 gigabyte dataset went up separately by file copy, since it is too large for git. The first attempt to initialize CUDA failed outright: the installed PyTorch wheel was built for a newer CUDA than the server's driver, which tops out at 12.2. Reinstalling the build for CUDA 12.1 fixed it. The run then went into a tmux session so it would survive the SSH connection dropping, with the loss piped to a log file.

For a while the log looked empty and the run looked stuck. It was neither. Piping through a file makes Python buffer its standard output, so the per-epoch lines sat in a buffer instead of reaching the file, while the GPU sat at 92 percent the whole time. The dataset, a million sequences loaded into memory at once, made each epoch slow, and thirty epochs took close to six hours overnight.

### The result, and the number that explained it

The run finished cleanly, and the result was bad. The loss had barely moved across thirty epochs, from 0.7388 to 0.6856, and that floor is the tell. The value 0.686 is almost exactly the natural logarithm of two, which is the loss of a model that predicts a fair coin and ignores its input. The evaluation confirmed it. Bit Error Rate 0.5859, Block Error Rate 1.0000, Hamming Loss 0.5859. The model was worse than random, and far worse than doing nothing.

The phrase "worse than doing nothing" became measurable. A baseline that simply copies the edited input to the output, scored with the same length-aware alignment, gives a Bit Error Rate of 0.0823 over five thousand samples. The channel only corrupts about eight percent of the information. Our trained model was seven times worse than a bare wire. Whatever was wrong, it was not the data being too hard.

A look at the length profile sharpened the picture and, in passing, vindicated the choice of CTC. The originals are always length 100, but the edited sequences run from 89 to 115, and only about thirteen percent share the original's length. So insertions and deletions are everywhere. The equal-length pairs carry around thirty positional mismatches each, yet the true edit distance is only about eight. That gap is the shift effect again, seen from the data side: a handful of indels create dozens of positional errors. A position-by-position model would drown in it, which is exactly why the alignment-based CTC loss was the right instinct.

So the framework was right and the model still collapsed. The deciding test was whether it could learn at all. A small overfitting run took 128 samples, raised the learning rate tenfold, added gradient clipping, and tried to memorize them. A healthy network drives a set that small to near-zero error in a few hundred steps. This one stuck at a Bit Error Rate of 0.5022 and would not move, while the loss only crawled. That rules out the learning rate and points straight at the architecture. The most likely culprit is the nearest-neighbor upsampling. Duplicating each frame produces identical adjacent vectors, and identical frames cannot express different decisions, so the set of alignments the decoder can represent is far narrower than the lattice length suggests. The blanks have room, but the model cannot use it.

### The call

The project had a deadline measured in hours, and a real fix meant changing the upsampling and retraining, which on a million sequences is another multi-hour run with no guarantee. The honest move was to stop, write the failure down with its diagnosis, and deliver the result that already stood: the Siamese model, with Hamming Loss marked not applicable. The Connectionist Temporal Classification work became the future-work section, with the learned upsampling layer named as the next step.

## What the three phases add up to

The thread through all of it is one tradeoff. Generation gives you bits and therefore every metric, but on a shifting channel a positional loss makes it fail as a decoder. Embedding sidesteps the shift and gives the best distance estimates, but it gives up bits and the metrics that need them. Connectionist Temporal Classification is the design that should bridge the two, and it still might, once the upsampling stops flattening the representation it depends on. The encoder was never the problem. Across all three phases, attention learned the geometry of edit operations well. What changed each time was the head bolted onto it and the metrics that head made possible.
